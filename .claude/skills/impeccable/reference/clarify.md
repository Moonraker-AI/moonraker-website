Identify and improve unclear, jargon-heavy, or off-voice copy on Moonraker pages so a worried first-time prospect understands instantly and feels at ease.

> **Additional context needed**: which client (their `voice_dna` may set the register), what page, and which copy specifically — or full surface scan.

## What "clear" means for Moonraker

Therapist-facing audience reads these pages, but the *target reader* is their prospective client. That reader is usually:

- Worried, sometimes in crisis, sometimes embarrassed about needing therapy.
- On their phone, reading at 11pm.
- Comparing two or three therapists across tabs.
- Skeptical of polished marketing language.
- Looking for one specific signal: "would I feel comfortable talking to this person?"

Clarity for that reader means warm, plain English. Not corporate. Not jargon. Not clinical. Not bubbly. Specifically:

- Short sentences when possible. Long sentences are fine when they need to be.
- "I" and "you," not "the therapist" and "clients."
- Concrete names for things ("anxiety," "couples therapy") not abstractions ("therapeutic modalities").
- Acknowledge the reader's situation when relevant ("If you're reading this at 2am, you're not alone.").
- No em-dashes anywhere. Use commas, colons, periods, or restructure. This is non-negotiable.

If the client has a stated voice in `design_specs.voice_dna`, layer that on top — but the warmth/plainness baseline holds.

## Assess Current Copy

For every text element on the page:

1. **Read it as the reader, not the writer**. Could a 19-year-old with no therapy experience read this and know what's being offered?
2. **Find clarity problems**:
   - **Therapy jargon**: "psychodynamic," "modalities," "intersubjective," "evidence-based" without a translation.
   - **Marketing jargon**: "transform your life," "unlock your potential," "discover your authentic self" — generic and AI-flavored.
   - **Vague verbs**: "explore," "address," "work with" without specifying the work.
   - **Passive voice when active is warmer**: "Your concerns will be heard" → "I listen carefully."
   - **Hedging**: "we believe" / "we strive to" / "may help" — confidence reassures.
   - **Length problems**: a 60-word sentence; a 3-word headline that omits the offer.
   - **Tone mismatches**: cheerful copy on the cancellation policy; corporate copy on the "About me" page.
   - **Em-dashes**: any em-dash ( — or –) is a defect. Find and replace.
3. **Understand the reader's mental state at this exact element**:
   - Hero on homepage → anxious, scanning, deciding whether to stay.
   - Service page → comparing, looking for "do they help with [my problem]."
   - About → curious about the person, looking for warmth and credibility.
   - Booking CTA → ready to act, needs zero friction.
   - FAQ → has a specific worry, wants a direct answer.

**CRITICAL**: clear copy helps the reader succeed. Unclear copy creates anxiety, doubt, and abandonment. On a therapy site, anxiety is what we're trying to *reduce*.

## Plan the Copy Changes

Before rewriting:

- **Primary message**: what's the one thing the reader needs to know from this element?
- **Action**: what should they do next, or feel?
- **Tone**: appropriate for this moment? (homepage hero = welcoming; cancellation page = empathetic-but-clear; booking CTA = confident).
- **Constraints**: H1 typically wraps to 2 lines max on mobile; CTA buttons typically 4-5 words; meta descriptions 150-160 chars.

## Improve Copy Systematically

### Hero Headlines

**Bad**: "Transform your life with therapy" (generic, vague, AI-flavored).
**Good**: "Therapy for adults navigating anxiety, burnout, and big life transitions, in [City]."

**Principles**:

- Name the offer concretely (what kind of therapy, for whom).
- Include the city if local — therapists rank locally and the reader is searching locally.
- Avoid generic transformational language.
- 2 lines max on mobile; aim for under 12 words.

### Subheads and Intro Copy

**Bad**: "We provide a safe and supportive environment to explore your thoughts and feelings."
**Good**: "I help adults work through anxiety, depression, and the messy in-between. Sessions are virtual or in-person at my [City] office."

**Principles**:

- Speak as the therapist (I/you), not the practice (we/clients).
- Specify who the work is for and what format (virtual, in-person, both).
- One concrete detail (location, modality, schedule) builds more trust than three abstractions.

### Service Descriptions

**Bad**: "Anxiety Therapy: Working through anxiety using evidence-based modalities tailored to your needs."
**Good**: "Anxiety: When worry takes over your day, your sleep, or your relationships, we'll work together to find what's driving it and what helps quiet it. I draw on CBT, IFS, and somatic approaches, depending on what fits you."

**Principles**:

- Lead with the reader's experience, not the technique.
- Name the techniques only after acknowledging the experience.
- Keep techniques unjargoned — translate or define.

### About / Bio

**Bad**: "Dr. [Name] is a licensed marriage and family therapist with over 15 years of experience helping clients achieve their therapeutic goals."
**Good**: "I'm [Name], a marriage and family therapist in [City]. I've been doing this work for fifteen years, mostly with couples in stuck patterns and individuals trying to figure out what they actually want."

**Principles**:

- First person, immediately.
- Concrete details (years, location, specialty).
- A sentence that hints at personality or perspective is worth ten "passionate about helping" lines.

### Button & CTA Text

**Bad**: "Submit" | "Click here" | "Get Started" | "Learn More" .
**Good**: "Book a free consultation" | "See available times" | "Email [Name]" | "Read about my approach".

**Principles**:

- Verb + specific object. No standalone verbs.
- Match the action's actual outcome ("Book" should book; "Learn more" should reveal more).
- Length: 2-5 words ideal.

### FAQ Answers

**Bad**: "We offer a sliding scale based on financial need."
**Good**: "Yes. My standard rate is $180 per session. If that's a stretch, I have a few sliding-scale spots between $100 and $150. Email me to ask."

**Principles**:

- Answer the question first, in the first six words.
- Concrete numbers reassure.
- Direct invitation to follow up beats hedged "contact us for details."

### Empty States

**Bad**: "No items yet."
**Good**: "Blog posts about therapy, anxiety, and relationships are coming soon. In the meantime, the most common questions I get are answered in the FAQ below."

**Principles**:

- Explain why empty.
- Offer the next best thing.
- Never end on a dead end.

### Error Messages

**Bad**: "An error occurred."
**Good**: "We couldn't send that — please try again, or email [therapist email] directly."

**Principles**:

- Plain language.
- Suggest the fix.
- Always offer a fallback path (an email, a phone number).
- Don't blame the user.

### Booking / Intake Copy

**Bad**: "Schedule your initial appointment now."
**Good**: "Book a free 15-minute consultation. We'll talk briefly to see if I'm the right fit for what you're working on."

**Principles**:

- Set expectations (length, free, what happens).
- Lower the activation energy ("free," "brief").
- Acknowledge the "fit" question explicitly — it's what the reader is wondering anyway.

## Apply Clarity Principles

For every rewrite:

1. **Be specific**: name the thing.
2. **Be concise** without being curt.
3. **Be active**: "I help" not "help is provided."
4. **Be human**: write like a real person who happens to be a therapist.
5. **Be helpful**: tell the reader what to do.
6. **Be consistent**: pick one term per concept and lock it in across the page (and across the site).
7. **No em-dashes**.

## Voice Cross-Check

If `design_specs.voice_dna` exists for this client, after rewriting, sanity-check each change against it. Two cases:

- **Voice DNA matches the warm-plain baseline**: ship.
- **Voice DNA is more formal / more clinical / more academic**: nudge the rewrite in that direction without losing the warmth-and-plain-English baseline. Therapy clients hire us partly *because* their existing voice is too cold; we don't preserve coldness.

If `voice_dna` is empty (Pagemaster pipeline didn't capture it, or analyze produced nothing usable), proceed on the baseline and flag it as a follow-up for `analyze-design-spec` re-run.

## Verify Improvements

For each rewrite:

- **Comprehension**: would the 19-year-old with no therapy experience understand it?
- **Actionability**: does the reader know what to do next, where applicable?
- **Brevity**: is it as short as it can be while staying clear?
- **Consistency**: matches terminology used elsewhere on the site?
- **Tone**: matches the reader's mental state at this element?
- **No em-dashes**: grep the file. None.

## When to push back

If the client provided copy that fails clarity but they're attached to it, flag it to Karen rather than rewriting unilaterally. Common case: a therapist's existing bio that they're proud of but uses heavy jargon. The right move is a conversation, not a stealth rewrite.

**NEVER**:

- Use jargon without a translation.
- Use em-dashes.
- Blame the reader in error copy.
- Be vague in CTA text.
- Use placeholders as labels.
- Vary terminology for variety.
- Soften clarity to sound "professional."

Remember: warm, plain, confident. The reader is anxious; your job is to lower that anxiety with every sentence.
